Overview

The GERIP program places participants inside active business and technology environments where cybersecurity threats are real, consequences are significant, and mitigation work happens daily. Participants are embedded with enterprise-facing research teams to identify, analyze, and help address the security vulnerabilities that organizations in the region face — from AI system exposures to data governance failures to network-level risk. This is hands-on, deliverable-driven work, not a classroom exercise. Participants select a placement site based on their field interests: Singapore through the National University of Singapore's School of Computing, working closely with regional industry partners; or Barcelona through the Autonomous University of Barcelona, embedded in Europe's AI regulatory and enterprise security ecosystem. Both sites offer direct mentorship from University of Maryland faculty and host institution researchers, with structured output expectations from week one.

Responsibilities
Cybersecurity Research & Analysis
  • Conduct literature reviews on AI-related cybersecurity threats including adversarial machine learning, prompt injection, model poisoning, and deepfake-enabled disinformation
  • Assist in documenting and categorizing known vulnerabilities in AI systems and agentic pipelines, referencing frameworks such as MITRE ATLAS and OWASP Top 10 for LLMs
  • Support threat modeling exercises by compiling attacker profiles, attack vectors, and case studies relevant to ongoing lab projects
  • Maintain a running database of security incidents, CVEs, and policy developments related to AI and media infrastructure
  • Contribute to red-team documentation — writing up findings, reproducing reported exploits in sandboxed environments, and summarizing results for the research team
Technical AI Systems Support
  • Assist in developing and testing agentic AI workflows with a focus on identifying failure modes, security gaps, and unintended behaviors in deployed systems
  • Run structured evaluations of AI tools — documenting outputs, flagging anomalies, and tracking performance across versions
  • Support data pipeline tasks including cleaning, labeling, and preparing datasets with attention to data integrity and privacy compliance
  • Help build and maintain internal technical documentation including system architecture notes, API usage logs, and tool evaluation reports
  • Assist with Python scripting and automation to streamline repetitive research workflows such as bulk data collection, parsing, and formatting
Research & Documentation
  • Support qualitative and quantitative research workflows across active lab projects — study design, source verification, and structured note-taking
  • Build and maintain annotated bibliographies covering AI security, polarization, media manipulation, and related policy fields
  • Compile thematic research briefs drawing on government reports, academic publications, and vetted news sources with attention to regional and U.S. regulatory developments
  • Draft internal memos and working documents that distill technical findings into accessible language for cross-disciplinary team members
Technical Communications & Outreach
  • Draft and edit external-facing content — blog posts, explainers, social media threads, and newsletters — translating technical security research for non-specialist audiences
  • Conduct user interviews and field tests to gather feedback on AI tools and research prototypes, then synthesize results into structured reports
  • Design visual communication assets such as threat diagrams, data flow charts, and infographics that make security concepts approachable and shareable

Placement Site Selection & Why These Locations

Participants select one of two placement sites during the application process based on their research interests and career focus. Both sites represent active University of Maryland international partnerships and are located in countries that are global leaders in technology investment, cybersecurity policy, and AI governance.

🇸🇬 Singapore

National University of Singapore · School of Computing

Singapore is one of the most technologically advanced nations in the world and ranks consistently in the top three globally for cybersecurity readiness, according to the ITU Global Cybersecurity Index. The city-state is home to over 80 of the world's top 100 technology companies, including Google, Meta, and Alibaba, which have all established regional headquarters and AI research centers there. Singapore's government has invested over $1 billion SGD in its National AI Strategy, making it Asia-Pacific's most active state actor in AI governance and deployment.

The cybersecurity sector alone employs over 10,000 professionals and is projected to grow significantly through 2030, driven by Singapore's role as a regional financial hub and its position as the primary gateway for data flows across Southeast Asia. The Cyber Security Agency of Singapore (CSA) is internationally recognized and works directly with institutions like NUS on threat intelligence and national security research — giving GERIP participants direct proximity to one of the most active public-sector cybersecurity environments in the world.

For students, Singapore offers unmatched professional exposure. The city hosts major annual events including the Singapore International Cyber Week (SICW), one of Asia's largest cybersecurity conferences, and the Asia Tech x Singapore (ATxSG) summit. Participants regularly leave with professional contacts spanning government, Big Tech, and regional startups — a network that is difficult to replicate from a U.S. campus. The National University of Singapore is ranked #1 in Asia and top 11 globally, with a School of Computing that produces research directly cited in international AI and security policy.

For the University of Maryland, the NUS partnership opens direct access to ASEAN-wide technology governance networks, government-affiliated security research, and the Asia-Pacific regulatory landscape — a region increasingly central to U.S. foreign policy on technology and digital trade.

🇪🇸 Barcelona, Spain

Autonomous University of Barcelona · Department of Computer Science

Barcelona has emerged as one of Europe's most dynamic technology and startup ecosystems, ranking among the top five European cities for tech investment and consistently attracting major AI and cybersecurity firms. The city is home to over 1,200 active tech startups and hosts the Mobile World Congress (MWC) — the world's largest annual technology event — which brings over 100,000 professionals and executives from more than 200 countries every year. For participants, simply being in Barcelona during the program period means proximity to a level of global industry activity that few academic settings can match.

On the cybersecurity and AI side, Barcelona is increasingly significant because of its position within the European Union's regulatory framework. The EU AI Act — the world's first comprehensive legal framework governing artificial intelligence — is now in effect, making EU-based researchers among the most sought-after voices in global AI policy. Companies and governments worldwide are actively seeking professionals who understand how to operate within GDPR and EU AI Act compliance environments, and hands-on research experience from within the EU is a direct differentiator on the job market. UAB's Department of Computer Science has active collaborations with the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) and contributes to NATO-affiliated security research initiatives.

Barcelona also serves as a bridge between the U.S. and European technology markets. Major firms including Amazon Web Services, King, Glovo, and Typeform have significant operations in the city, and the broader Catalonia region hosts Spain's largest concentration of AI research centers. For students with interests in privacy law, AI ethics, or enterprise security, the Barcelona site offers a research environment that directly mirrors where the global conversation on AI governance is happening in real time.

For the University of Maryland, the UAB partnership provides a direct pipeline into European regulatory networks and transatlantic cybersecurity policy forums — positioning the program at the intersection of U.S. and EU approaches to AI governance, an area of growing institutional priority.

GERIP is one of the most selective international research programs offered through the University of Maryland iSchool. With only five spots per site, the program is intentionally small — every participant is assigned a named faculty mentor, attends all lab meetings as a full contributor, and leaves with documented research output. Selection is competitive across three elimination rounds before a final offer is extended.

1

Written Application ~250 applicants advance

Submit a resume, unofficial transcript, a 400-word personal statement addressing your interest in AI security research and how this placement connects to your academic and career goals, and a completed site preference form indicating Singapore or Barcelona with a written rationale. Applications missing any component are automatically disqualified.

2

Technical Writing Sample ~80 applicants advance

Submit one technical writing sample (500–800 words) — a lab report, security analysis, research summary, or documentation you have authored independently. Reviewers assess technical accuracy, clarity for a non-specialist audience, and evidence of structured analytical thinking. AI-generated submissions are disqualified upon detection.

3

Faculty Review & Shortlist 30 candidates shortlisted

Complete applications are reviewed by a panel of four faculty supervisors — Dr. Mazurek, Dr. Vitak, Dr. Bulusu, and Dr. Chetty — alongside the iSchool Global Programs Office. Each application receives independent scores across five criteria: technical preparation, research literacy, communication quality, site fit, and academic standing. The top 30 candidates across both sites are advanced to interview.

4

Interview Process — 5 Parts 30 candidates → 10 offers

The interview process is conducted in five sequential parts over two sessions. All parts are required. Candidates who do not complete every part are not considered for final offers.

Session 1 — Virtual (60 min total)

Part 1 · Background & Motivation (15 min)

Conducted by one University of Maryland faculty supervisor. Covers academic background, relevant coursework or certifications, and the candidate's specific motivation for their chosen placement site. Reviewers assess clarity of purpose and fit with the program's business-focused research agenda.

Part 2 · Technical Problem-Solving (20 min)

Candidates are presented with a real-world scenario — a business that has suffered a security incident or an AI system with a suspected vulnerability — and asked to reason through the problem live. No memorized answers accepted; reviewers are evaluating analytical process, not just conclusions. Basic familiarity with frameworks such as MITRE or OWASP is expected.

Part 3 · Communication & Translation Exercise (25 min)

Candidates are given a brief technical finding — a short vulnerability summary or security assessment excerpt — and asked to explain it as if presenting to a non-technical business executive. This section tests the candidate's ability to communicate risk in plain language, a critical skill for any professional working at the interface of security and business leadership.

Session 2 — Panel (55 min total) · Top 15 candidates only

Part 4 · Research Pitch (10 min)

Candidates prepare and deliver a 7–10 minute structured pitch on a cybersecurity or AI security challenge relevant to their chosen placement site — either a current threat facing businesses in Singapore or a policy or compliance issue in the Barcelona/EU context. The panel evaluates depth of understanding, quality of sourcing, and how clearly the candidate frames the problem and potential approaches. Slides are optional but not required.

Part 5 · Panel Q&A (45 min)

A three-person panel — two University of Maryland faculty supervisors and one representative from the host institution (NUS or UAB) — conducts a structured interview covering cross-cultural professional readiness, response to ambiguous or high-pressure scenarios, ethical decision-making in security contexts, and program-specific questions about the candidate's planned contributions. This session is designed to be challenging; candidates are expected to think carefully, acknowledge uncertainty when appropriate, and demonstrate professional maturity.

5

Background Review & Final Offer 10 offers extended

Final candidates undergo an academic standing and conduct review conducted by the iSchool Global Programs Office. Official offer letters are issued within 10 business days. Accepted participants must confirm within 5 business days and immediately begin the pre-departure workshop enrollment process. Declining after confirmation forfeits the spot to the waitlist.
